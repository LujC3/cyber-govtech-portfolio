  EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

  The Philippines ranks 65th globally in AI readiness — behind
  Singapore (2nd), Malaysia (23rd), Thailand (37th), Indonesia
  (42nd), and Vietnam (59th) within ASEAN alone. While the
  government has adopted multiple AI roadmaps and strategies, the
  country lacks a binding legal framework for AI governance, a
  dedicated institutional authority, and a risk classification
  system for AI applications.

  As 2026 ASEAN Chair, the Philippines has both the opportunity
  and obligation to advance regional AI governance — but credible
  regional leadership requires domestic institutional readiness.

  This brief identifies 10 governance gaps, benchmarks them
  against ASEAN peers and global leaders, and proposes a phased
  policy roadmap for Philippine AI governance.

  GOVERNANCE GAPS (10 DIMENSIONS)

  ●●●●●  Legal Framework
           Current: No dedicated AI law; reliance on DPA 2012 and E-Governance Act (RA 12254)
           Gap:     No binding legal framework specifically governing AI development or deployment
           Bench:   Vietnam enacted AI Law No. 134/2025; EU AI Act operational since 2024

  ●●●●○  Institutional Architecture
           Current: Multiple agencies (DICT, DOST, DEPDev) with overlapping AI mandates
           Gap:     No single coordinating authority for AI governance; fragmented jurisdiction
           Bench:   Singapore has dedicated AI agency (AISG); Thailand has NSTDA AI Center

  ●●●●●  Risk Classification
           Current: No AI risk classification system in Philippine law or regulation
           Gap:     No mechanism to distinguish high-risk AI from low-risk applications
           Bench:   EU AI Act tiered risk (unacceptable/high/limited/minimal); ASEAN Guide recommends risk-based approach

  ●●●●●  Data Infrastructure
           Current: OECD Digital Government Index score 0.28/1.0 (third lowest in ASEAN-8)
           Gap:     Government data systems are fragmented; limited interoperability; no national data strategy
           Bench:   Singapore scores 0.74; ASEAN average 0.37

  ●●●●○  Workforce Capacity
           Current: SPARTA platform and DICT training programs exist but underscaled
           Gap:     Insufficient AI-literate civil servants; no AI competency framework for government
           Bench:   South Korea requires AI literacy for all senior officials; Singapore GovTech academy

  ●●●●○  Algorithmic Transparency
           Current: No requirement for explainability or audit of government AI systems
           Gap:     Citizens have no mechanism to challenge AI-assisted government decisions
           Bench:   EU AI Act requires explanation for high-risk decisions; NZ Algorithm Charter

  ●●●○○  Ethical Guidelines
           Current: NAISR 2.0 includes ethical principles but they are non-binding
           Gap:     No enforceable ethical standards; no ethics review process for government AI
           Bench:   ASEAN Guide provides principles; Thailand has AI ethics committee

  ●●●●○  Public Sector AI Adoption
           Current: Pilot projects (DICT chatbots, DOST research) but no systematic deployment
           Gap:     No procurement framework for AI solutions; no standards for government AI
           Bench:   Indonesia Pancasila-grounded AI in public services; Malaysia MyDigital initiative

  ●●●○○  International Alignment
           Current: Signatory to ASEAN Guide; participant in UNESCO assessment; 2026 ASEAN Chair
           Gap:     Strong declaratory commitments but weak domestic implementation
           Bench:   Singapore leads ASEAN implementation; Brunei aligned national guide to ASEAN in 2025

  ●●●●○  Cybersecurity Integration
           Current: Cybercrime Prevention Act (RA 10175) and DICT cybersecurity mandate exist
           Gap:     AI-specific cybersecurity risks (adversarial attacks, deepfakes) not addressed in current law
           Bench:   DICT Grok takedown order (2026) showed reactive capability but no proactive framework

------------------------------------------------------------------------

  POLICY RECOMMENDATIONS

  PHASE 1 — IMMEDIATE (2026)
  ──────────────────────────
  1. Finalize and publish the DEPDev AI Governance Framework
     as announced at the 2026 National Innovation Day.

  2. Designate a single coordinating authority for AI governance
     (recommended: DEPDev, with DICT as implementing agency).

  3. Issue an Executive Order requiring all government agencies
     to inventory existing and planned AI deployments.

  PHASE 2 — SHORT-TERM (2026-2027)
  ────────────────────────────────
  4. Draft a Philippine AI Governance Act adopting risk-based
     classification aligned with the ASEAN Guide and EU AI Act
     tiered model.

  5. Develop a Philippine Government Data Strategy to raise
     the OECD Digital Government score from 0.28 toward the
     ASEAN average of 0.37.

  6. Adopt a Philippine Algorithm Charter modeled on New
     Zealand's, requiring transparency for government AI.

  7. Mandate AI literacy modules in CSC career development
     programs for all senior government officials.

  PHASE 3 — MEDIUM-TERM (2027-2028)
  ─────────────────────────────────
  8. Establish a National AI Ethics Board with review authority
     over high-risk government AI applications.

  9. Issue DBM-GPPB procurement guidelines for AI solutions
     under RA 9184, including explainability and bias testing
     requirements.

  10. Amend RA 10175 (Cybercrime Prevention Act) to address
      AI-specific threats: adversarial attacks, deepfakes,
      and automated social engineering.

  THE ASEAN CHAIR OPPORTUNITY

  The Philippines assumed the ASEAN Chairmanship on 1 January
  2026 under the theme "Navigating Our Future Together." This
  provides a unique window to:

  • Champion the ASEAN Responsible AI Roadmap 2025-2030
  • Propose harmonized AI risk classification across ASEAN
  • Host an ASEAN AI Governance Summit (Cebu or Manila)
  • Demonstrate domestic implementation as credibility signal

  Regional leadership without domestic readiness produces
  declarations without implementation. The domestic framework
  must advance in parallel with ASEAN commitments.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

  COMPARATIVE NOTE: NEW ZEALAND

  New Zealand offers a relevant benchmark as a small democracy
  with strong institutional capacity. Its Algorithm Charter
  (voluntary, adopted by 30+ government agencies) demonstrates
  that transparency norms can be implemented without binding
  legislation — a pragmatic model for the Philippines, where
  legislative processes are slow and institutional capacity
  is uneven. However, binding legislation will ultimately be
  necessary as AI adoption deepens in government services.
